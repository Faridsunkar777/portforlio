Design Process

Discover

Problem Statement

Dreams are important, especially when we are young. Many children discover they have color vision deficiency (CVD) too late—often when pursuing careers like medicine or aviation. This late discovery shatters dreams and leaves individuals feeling lost about their future. The challenge is to help parents identify CVD early in their children's lives, allowing kids to explore alternative passions and dreams before career paths are set.

Research

Background Research

I conducted desk research on colorblindness and discovered that many people find out about it late in their lives, especially when trying to find their career paths. I found out there are many different types of color blindness, and I needed to understand how to help all of them. I looked for doctors to find out how they conduct tests and learned about the best methods for testing CVD patients.

Research

User Interviews

I conducted in-depth interviews with individuals who discovered they have color vision deficiency later in life, particularly those who aspired to careers like medicine or aviation. Through conversations about their experiences, dreams, and how early detection might have changed their paths, I gained deep insights into their emotional journeys. The interviews revealed common themes around shattered dreams, feelings of lost potential, and the importance of early detection. Most importantly, participants emphasized that children have amazing imagination and curiosity, which is the perfect time to explore alternative passions. These findings directly informed the app's concept and design approach.

Define

Findings

Through interviews, I found that people with CVD often discover it when they get to university and want to pursue their dreams. Most wanted to be doctors and pilots. When asked if they knew earlier, how would it change their lives, the response was: "If I knew earlier, it would help me find other passions early and have other dreams, because children have amazing imagination and that's when they are the most curious about life." This sparked the idea to create an app for parents to find out if their kids have CVD early through a game.

Develop

LO-FI Wireframes

I learned that the best method for testing CVD is through shapes and numbers—if they can identify them with the colors that are in the spectrum. I got the idea of creating a game that tests kids with shapes and called it "Peek-A-Shape." Why a game? Because I wanted the kids to feel less intimidated.

Test

User Testing

I conducted usability testing sessions with parents and their children using the low-fidelity wireframes of the game. Parents were asked to guide their children through the testing process, while children interacted with the shape identification game. The testing revealed that children engaged better with game-like interfaces, but some shapes and instructions needed to be clearer. Parents also expressed the need for more detailed results and guidance on next steps. Based on these findings, I refined the wireframes to improve clarity, simplify instructions, and enhance the overall testing experience before moving to high-fidelity designs.

Iterate

Design Iterations

Based on the user testing feedback, I iterated on the wireframes multiple times. I simplified the shape identification process, made instructions more child-friendly with visual cues, and added a progress indicator so children could see how far along they were in the game. I also incorporated colorful animations to make the experience more engaging and rewarding. Parents appreciated the addition of a detailed results dashboard that explained the findings in simple terms and provided actionable next steps for seeking professional evaluation.

Define

User Personas

I created a persona to guide Ayu Fahrela, a concerned mother of a 5-year-old boy who wants to ensure early detection of any visual challenges, Understanding both the parent's need for accurate testing and the child's need for an engaging, non-intimidating experience was crucial in balancing educational value with playful interaction.

Develop

I.A

I structured the app flow to ensure a seamless experience: parents start with a simple onboarding process that explains the purpose and how the test works, followed by child profile creation, then the game begins with easy-to-follow instructions. The testing phase includes multiple rounds of shape identification with varying difficulty levels, and finally, a comprehensive results page with recommendations. This clear structure ensures parents feel confident guiding their children through the process.

Deliver

High-Fidelity Design

For the high-fidelity designs, I chose bright, playful colors that appeal to children while maintaining scientific accuracy for CVD testing. I used rounded shapes and friendly illustrations to create a welcoming environment. The typography is large and clear, making it easy for young children to read. Interactive elements include smooth animations and sound effects that provide positive reinforcement. The parent dashboard features a clean, professional design with data visualizations that make results easy to understand.

Impact

Impact & Results

The app has shown promising results in early testing phases. Parents reported that their children enjoyed the game-like experience and didn't realize they were being tested. The accuracy of CVD detection aligned well with professional optometric assessments. Several parents discovered their children had mild CVD that would have gone undetected for years. Most importantly, early detection has enabled these families to have important conversations about career planning and passion exploration at an age when children are most open to discovering new interests. The app has helped shift the narrative from "shattered dreams" to "redirected potential."

Reflection

Reflection

Through an iterative design process, I developed Peek-A-Shape, a game that helps parents identify color vision deficiency early in their children's lives. By making it a game, children feel less intimidated and are more engaged in the testing process. This early detection allows kids to explore alternative passions and dreams before career paths are set, helping them find other things to do while maintaining their curiosity and imagination. This project taught me the importance of designing with empathy, balancing scientific accuracy with user experience, and creating solutions that can have a meaningful impact on people's lives.
